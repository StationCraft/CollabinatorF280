This Standard provides contractors, equipment manufacturers, building officials, and others involved in
residential construction with updated calculation methods for determining the output capacity of all
types of applicable residential space heating and cooling appliances to maintain specified indoor
environmental conditions in Canadian homes. However, this Standard does not cover the detailed
design and installation of residential space heating and cooling systems.
Microsoft Excel® spreadsheets are linked to this Standard for calculating heat loss and heat gain
associated with all types of common foundations. The spreadsheets can also be used to calculate some

description of the spreadsheets, along with instructions for accessing them, is provided in Annex F.
Instructions for the use of the spreadsheets can also be found within the spreadsheets themselves.
Additional changes to this edition include calculations for determining heat loss due to continuous
ventilation, air change heat loss for each room, heat gain through transparent and translucent building
assemblies, and heat gain adjustments for shading.
Annex B provides a commentary on specific clauses in the Standard with additional information on the
principles used for the modelling of foundations, air leakage, and windows. Annex B is also intended to
outline, where they are not self-evident, the intent of and rationale behind the various requirements of
this Standard in order to facilitate understanding and application of those requirements.

1 Scope
1.1
This Standard provides a calculation method for determining the heat loss and heat gain of buildings (as
described in Clause 1.3 below) for the purpose of selecting the appropriate output capacity of a space
heating appliance or group of appliances and the output capacity of a cooling appliance or group of
appliances.
1.2
This Standard also provides restrictions on selecting the output capacity of a specific space heating or
cooling appliance or group of appliances.
1.3
This Standard applies to space heating and cooling appliances for use in housing and small buildings of
residential occupancy to which Part 9 of the National Building Code of Canada applies, where the
appliances are permanently installed within the dwelling unit they serve.
3B) 1.3A
This Standard also provides a method for verifying whether a software system produces heat loss and
gain results that are representative of the results that would be obtained using a method that exactly
follows the calculation method set out in this Standard.
3) 1.3B
This Standard applies to software systems which are intended to be used to calculate heat loss and gain
of homes according to this Standard.
1.4
This Standard does not deal with detailed design and installation of space heating and cooling systems,
but is based on the assumption that the distribution system design complies with good engineering
practice as described in the applicable building codes and standards.
3) 1.5
The requirements of this Standard are expressed in SI (metric) units.

3 Definitions
(3) 3.1 General
The following definitions shall apply in this Standard:
Note: Defined terms are shown in italics wherever they occur in this Standard.
Air change — the sum of air leakage and ventilation.
Air leakage — the uncontrolled exchange of air between the interior and exterior environments
through unintentional openings in a building envelope under the influence of wind and buoyancy
pressures.
Note: /nfiltration and exfiltration are both components of air leakage.
Basement — the storey(s) of a building located below the first storey.
Bonus room — a large room in a house that could be used as a multi-purpose area.
Note: A bonus room, unlike an American bedroom, does not usually contain a closet. A bonus room might be used
as a family room, sewing or hobby room, game room, home cinema, office, or den. It is sometimes over a garage
or in an attic area with partially reduced ceiling height or some other less desirable characteristic. In some areas
the term may be used to describe a room that cannot legally be called a bedroom (because of inadequate ceiling
height, fire safety features, etc.).
Building envelope — the assembly of walls, ceilings, floors, windows, and doors that separates
conditioned space from the outdoors, from unconditioned space, or from the soil.
Conditioned space — any interior portion of a building that is intended to be heated or cooled.
First storey — the uppermost storey having its floor level not more than 2 m above grade.
Grade (as applying to the determination of building height) — the lowest of the average levels of
finished ground adjoining each exterior wall of a building, except that localized depressions, such as for
vehicle or pedestrian entrances, need not be considered in the determination of average levels of
finished ground. (See First storey.)
Note: Localized depressions, such as for vehicle or pedestrian entrances, need not be considered in the
determination of average levels of finished ground.
Heating system capacity — the total combined output capacity of all heating devices installed in a
building and intended to be operational at the design environmental conditions specified in Clause 4, as
rated at those design conditions.
Qualified operator — the person who operates the software to produce the reports to be used for the
verification report and who
a) has aminimum of 5 years’ experience operating the software which is being tested; or
b) has received specific training from the vendor of the software being tested and is approved by the
vendor of the software being tested as a qualified operator.
Software system — a software-based system for calculating the heating and cooling loads for a home
and for individual rooms within a home according to the method set out in this Standard.

Air films and spaces
Adjacent to the exposed surfaces of any building assembly are very thin films of still air that contribute
a small but appreciable amount of thermal resistance. The exterior film is assumed to contribute less,
due to the scouring action of wind. Air spaces within the assembly also contribute thermal resistance.
The exact amount of this contribution depends on the thickness and other dimensions of the space
(which affect the convective heat transfer), and on whether the space is faced with reflective material
(which affects the radiative heat transfer). The values given in Table 7B are somewhat simplified, but
adequate for the purposes of this Standard.

A common mistake in RSI value calculations is the assumption that reflective films contribute thermal
resistance in all building assemblies that incorporate them. In fact, reflective films contribute resistance
only when they are facing an air space, as it is only then that radiative heat transfer becomes at all
significant. Thus, a reflective film sandwiched between two other materials will make no significant
contribution to the thermal resistance of an assembly.

example, 150 mm batts may be used in a wall built with 38 x 140 mm studs. The resulting compression
reduces the thermal resistance of the batts, but the reduction in the RSI value is not proportionate to
the reduction in thickness. The graph below shows an approximate relationship between amount of
compression and reduction in RSI value. For example, a 150 mm batt compressed into a 140 mm cavity

In frame construction, the insulation is interrupted at regular intervals by the framing. The added heat
loss through the framing results in an appreciable reduction in the overall RSI value of a building

B.0 Introduction
In the past, standards and procedures for determining the required capacity of residential heating and
cooling systems have been developed by a number of agencies, many of whom were concerned with a
particular form of energy. This has led to a proliferation of procedures written in a variety of formats,
with differing methods and inputs that give diverse results. A study carried out for the Division of
Building Research of the National Research Council* indicated that
e each of the procedures then in use had its strengths and weaknesses;
e all of these procedures tended to result in the oversizing of heating systems, which is undesirable for
a number of reasons;
e the differences in final results were primarily due to differences in the treatment of air change heat
loss and below-grade heat loss; and
¢ none of these procedures took advantage of current research findings in the areas of air change heat
loss and below-grade heat loss.
This study was presented to the agencies responsible for many of the then-current procedures and to
representatives of the codes and standards community. Previous editions of this Standard were
developed to ensure a comprehensive calculation procedure that would apply to all common heating
and cooling systems, regardless of prime energy source, and that would address the above concerns.
The 2012 edition of this Standard was able to build on the principles of earlier versions with specific
advancements in how foundations, air leakage, and windows are modelled. These enhancements were
based largely on work that was done by Natural Resources Canada in their development of the current
versions of the HOT2000 Energy Simulation Software. Many changes in this edition of the Standard
were designed to reflect advancements in building science and the increasing energy efficiency of new
buildings and to ensure accurate sizing throughout a range of building ages, locations, and efficiency.
This Commentary is intended to outline, where they are not self-evident, the intent of and rationale
behind the requirements of this Standard in order to facilitate understanding and application of those
requirements.

B.3 Definitions
Commentary is provided for the following definitions:
Air change, air leakage, and ventilation
This Standard departs from previous practice in that the term “air leakage” is used rather than the
more common, but less precise, “infiltration”. A distinction has also been made between “air leakage”
and “ventilation”. This definition of ventilation, with its emphasis on deliberateness, precludes the
common mistake of considering uncontrolled air leakage to be “natural ventilation”.
Heating system capacity
This definition is straightforward enough, except for the phrase “intended to be operational at the
design environmental conditions specified in Clause 4....” The purpose of this phrase is to exclude from
the calculation the capacity of any heat pump that forms part of a heating system designed to depend
exclusively on auxiliary heat at the outdoor design temperature. In such a case, only the output of the
auxiliary heating equipment would constitute the heating system capacity. Of course, if the heat pump
were intended for operation at the outdoor design temperature, its output at that temperature would
be included in the heating system capacity calculation along with the output of the auxiliary heating
equipment.
Unfinished basement
It is necessary to use this term for compatibility with the National Building Code of Canada in terms of
interior design temperatures. However, it is not possible to take the apparently logical step of relating
the definition to the degree of finishing present. The requirement of many building codes that
foundation walls be insulated has lead to such walls having a “finished” appearance, even in spaces not
intended to be used as living space. Although there is some hazard in assuming that the designer of the
space conditioning system understands the intentions of the building designer or owner, it is usually
clear enough that spaces such as furnace rooms and storage rooms are not intended for prolonged
occupancy.

considers the interaction between air leakage and ventilation, but the resulting value from the

and exhaust ventilation rates are equal, the resulting air leakage rates are the same as if there is no
ventilation. |f supply-biased or exhaust-biased ventilation rates are entered, however, the resulting air
leakage rates are reduced. This occurs because the underlying calculation predicts the amount by which
the air leakage will be reduced by the unbalanced ventilation. In the case of a relatively air-tight house
with a supply-biased or exhaust-biased ventilation system, the air leakage rates calculated by the
spreadsheet will approach zero because the mechanical ventilation system is driving almost all of the
air-change through the building envelope. The spreadsheet calculation does not add the ventilation rate

systems using radiant heating elements that are contained within building components (e.g., walls,
floors, or ceilings) show greater design load heat losses for individual spaces. This is due to the greater
temperature differential across that building component resulting from the radiant heat source. This
effect can be mitigated by designers encouraging building owners to increase levels of insulation
between the radiant heat source and the external environment.
